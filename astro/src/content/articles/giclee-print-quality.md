If you want an art print that looks as close to the original artwork as possible and stays beautiful for decades, giclee is the benchmark. It is not just a marketing word. It is a print process built around archival pigment inks, acid-free paper, and very high-resolution output.

## What makes a giclee print different

Giclee printing uses fine nozzles to place microscopic droplets of pigment ink with extreme precision. Combined with calibrated color profiles and high-resolution files, it reproduces subtle gradients, soft transitions, and the smallest details that are often lost in standard poster printing.

That is why giclee prints are the go-to choice for galleries, collectors, and artists who care about fidelity.

## Longevity you can trust

Pigment inks are designed for longevity. On archival, acid-free papers, they resist fading and color shift far better than dye-based prints. When displayed away from direct sunlight and stored well, giclee prints are often rated to last for many decades and, in some cases, 100+ years.

That means the print you frame today will look the same years from now.

## Precision and tonal depth

Giclee printers can hit a wide color gamut with deep blacks and smooth gradients. For generative and abstract work, that matters: you want clean transitions, no banding, and color accuracy across every layer.

In other words, the print keeps the intent of the artwork intact.

## Acid-free, museum-grade paper

The paper is just as important as the ink. Giclee prints are typically produced on acid-free, cotton rag or museum-grade papers. For example, I print on Hahnemuhle Photo Rag 308gsm matte paper, which is thick, softly textured, and made for archival work.

That texture adds a tactile depth you simply do not get with thin, glossy poster stock.
